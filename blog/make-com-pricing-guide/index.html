Make.com Pricing Guide 2026: Is It Worth It? (Honest Review)

📅 2026-07-23 ⏱ 5 min read ✍️ FlowStack Editorial

Make.com is worth it if you need a visual workflow builder with generous free operations and affordable paid tiers, but the per-operation pricing model means costs scale quickly as your automations grow—making it less suitable for high-volume use cases without budget planning.

  • Free plan: 1,000 operations/month, up to 2 active scenarios
  • Standard plan: $9.99/month for 10,000 operations; $19.99/month for 100,000
  • Pro plan: $29.99/month (200,000 ops) up to $299/month (2,000,000 ops)
  • Operations: Each workflow step counts as 1+ operation; bundled apps cost less than unbundled
  • No per-user fee: Unlimited team members on paid plans; only operation volume matters

What is Make.com and How Does Its Pricing Model Work?

Make.com (formerly Integromat) is a visual workflow automation platform that connects 1,000+ apps without requiring code. Unlike Zapier's per-task model or n8n's self-hosted flexibility, Make charges based on operations

The pricing model is straightforward: you pay for the number of operations your automations execute monthly. If your workflow has 5 steps and runs 100 times, that's 500 operations. For small to medium workloads, this approach is competitive. But for workflows that run frequently or have many steps, costs accumulate fast. There's no per-user fee, so adding team members doesn't increase your bill—only workflow execution volume does.

What Are Make.com's Current Plan Options and Limits?

Free plan includes 1,000 operations per month and supports up to 2 active scenarios. This is suitable for testing and light automation but hits limits quickly for production use.

Standard plans start at $9.99/month (10,000 operations) and scale to $19.99/month (100,000 operations). These are the sweet spot for most small businesses and solopreneurs.

Pro plans range from $29.99/month (200,000 operations) to $299/month (2,000,000 operations). At the highest tier, you're paying roughly $0.00015 per operation—but you need massive volume to justify the upfront cost.

Enterprise plans are custom-quoted and include dedicated support, priority processing, and advanced security features. Contact Make's sales team for details if you're running 2M+ operations monthly.

All paid plans include unlimited scenarios (workflows), unlimited team members, and phone/email support. The free plan has 1-week execution history; paid plans offer 30+ days of logs.

The per-operation cost decreases as you scale, but the absolute monthly spend grows. A workflow executing 500,000 times monthly costs at least $100. If that same workflow runs 1,000,000 times, you're at $199.99/month minimum. This is why estimating operation volume before committing to a plan is critical.

When Should You Upgrade Your Make.com Plan?

Upgrade to Standard ($9.99) immediately after exhausting your free 1,000 operations. The jump from free to paid is steep in terms of capability—you gain 10x the operations for roughly $10, which is a clear win.

Move to Standard ($19.99) if you're running 15,000–30,000 operations monthly. This plan handles light production automation—daily email digests, form submissions, or weekly data syncs—without overage fees.

Consider Pro ($29.99 or higher) if your monthly operations exceed 50,000. At this scale, you're likely managing multiple automations or running high-frequency workflows. The Pro tier is where Make becomes genuinely cost-effective for small teams and agencies.

Upgrade to Enterprise only if you're executing 2M+ operations monthly and need dedicated support, SLA guarantees, or advanced IP whitelisting. The custom pricing is transparent with sales, but assume $500+/month at these volumes.

Monitor your operation count monthly—Make's dashboard shows real-time usage. If you're consistently hitting 80%+ of your plan's limit, upgrade to avoid overages on the next billing cycle.

Make vs. Zapier: Make is cheaper if your workflows have multiple steps. Zapier charges per task (often 2–5 tasks per workflow step), so a 10-step workflow might cost 20–30 tasks. Make counts the same workflow as 10 operations. Zapier wins for single-trigger-single-action automation.

Make vs. n8n Cloud: n8n's cloud offering is comparable in price but designed for developers. Make's visual builder is more intuitive for non-technical users. n8n self-hosted is free but requires server management and technical setup.

Make vs. Pabbly Connect: Pabbly is slightly cheaper at the entry level but has fewer integrations (500+ vs. Make's 1,000+). For basic automation, Pabbly suffices. Make scales better as your needs grow.

Frequently Asked Questions

What counts as an operation in Make.com?

An operation is counted every time a module (action or trigger) executes. In a workflow with 5 steps, each execution costs 5 operations. Some app modules (like Google Sheets or Slack) are considered "bundled" and use 1 operation regardless of complexity. Unbundled apps (smaller or third-party integrations) always count as 1 operation per module. Errors, retries, and conditional branches that don't execute don't count—only completed steps.

Can I switch plans mid-month or get a refund?

Yes, you can upgrade or downgrade anytime. Make prorates billing—if you upgrade mid-month, you're charged the difference for the remaining days. Refunds are not offered for unused operations, but you can downgrade to lower your next month's charge. There's no cancellation penalty; you can pause your account or delete scenarios anytime.

Do I pay overages if I exceed my plan's operation limit?

No. Make.com pauses new workflow executions once you hit your monthly operation limit. You won't be charged overages, but your automations stop running until the month resets or you upgrade. Plan conservatively to avoid production outages near month-end.

Is Make.com worth it for high-volume automation?

For 1M+ monthly operations, Make becomes expensive (potentially $200+/month). At that scale, self-hosted n8n or a custom solution may be cheaper. For 50k–500k operations, Make is worth it because the platform requires no coding, scales reliably, and includes team collaboration features that competitor platforms charge extra for.
